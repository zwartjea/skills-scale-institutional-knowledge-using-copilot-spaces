# OctoAcme — Project Management Overview

OctoAcme uses a lightweight, iterative approach to project management that moves work from a short Project One‑pager through planning, execution, release, and retrospective. Initiation uses a one‑pager to capture the problem, SMART objectives, success metrics, stakeholders, and a high‑level timeline; approved initiatives move into planning where work is broken into shippable increments and a prioritized backlog with acceptance criteria is created. The lifecycle stages (Initiation → Planning → Execution → Release → Close & Retrospective) give teams clear decision gates and artifacts to keep work aligned and measurable. See the detailed guidance in the docs/ folder (e.g., octoacme-project-initiation.md and octoacme-project-planning.md).

Day‑to‑day workflows emphasize small, accountable batches and clear handoffs. Teams track work on a project board (Backlog → Ready → In Progress → In Review → QA → Done) and follow a pull request workflow that favors small PRs, links to issues and acceptance criteria, and requires CI (tests, linting, security scans) and approvals before merging. Releases are categorized (patch, minor, major) and follow pre‑release checks, deployment checklists, smoke tests, and a rollback plan to reduce risk and improve observability. See octoacme-execution-and-tracking.md and octoacme-release-and-deployment.md for checklists and release templates.

Roles and responsibilities are explicit so ownership is clear: Product Managers (PdM) define outcomes and success metrics and prioritize the backlog; Project Managers (PM) coordinate schedules, risks, and stakeholder communications; Developers implement and test solutions; QA validates acceptance criteria; and stakeholders provide inputs and approvals. The personas document (octoacme-roles-and-personas.md) describes typical communications and responsibilities for each role to reduce ambiguity and speed decision making.

Communication and quality assurance are built into the cadence and artifacts. The team rhythm includes daily standups for blockers, weekly delivery syncs for status and risks, sprint demos, and monthly stakeholder updates; incident communications and escalation paths are documented and captured in the Risk Register. QA practices require unit and integration tests, CI security scanning, end‑to‑end smoke tests for critical flows, and manual QA when needed; retrospectives convert learnings into tracked action items. See octoacme-risks-and-communication.md and octoacme-retrospective-and-continuous-improvement.md for templates and examples.
